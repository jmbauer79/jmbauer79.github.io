Paris

A return to one of the world’s great cities — full of museums, long walks, beautiful food, layered history, and the particular magic of seeing Paris with people you love.

November 2025 · Paris, France

Paris has a way of making even an ordinary walk feel composed. A street corner, a café table, a bridge over the Seine, the glow of a shop window in the late afternoon — everything seems to arrive with a little more drama and polish than it would anywhere else.

This trip was about seeing the city not just as a checklist of famous places, but as a place to wander through slowly. Paris rewards that kind of attention. The grandeur is obvious, but the smaller moments are often what linger: the pause between museum rooms, the first sip of coffee, the changing light on stone buildings, and the feeling of stepping into a city that has been imagined, painted, written about, and walked through by millions before you.

The Louvre was a highlight not only because of what it contains, but because of the way a good guide can make a massive museum feel human-scaled. Instead of being swallowed by the size of the place, we could follow a thread through it — art, history, power, beauty, ambition, and the long conversation between the past and the present.

A mesmerizing view from the top of the Arc de Triomphe: traffic slowly circling below, with the Eiffel Tower in the background.

Paris can feel overwhelming at first, but then one moment opens a door into another, and the city begins to feel personal.

What I love about travel is how quickly a place becomes attached to memory. The Louvre was not just the Louvre; it was the three of us standing together in that space, listening, looking, learning, and occasionally being amazed by how much history could be held in a single room.

Beyond the museum, Paris offered the pleasures that make the city feel inexhaustible: lingering meals, beautiful streets, grand views, quiet corners, and the steady sense that there was always one more thing worth noticing. Even when the itinerary was full, the best parts were often the transitions — walking from one place to another, letting the city fill in the spaces between plans.
