Jacumba Wilderness

An overnight hike into the desert wilderness near the border, where three friends followed rough trails, old stories, strange rocks, and the long quiet of the backcountry.

March 2022 · Jacumba Wilderness, California

Three of us set out for an overnight hike into the desert wilderness near Jacumba, close enough to San Diego to feel familiar, but far enough east to feel like we had crossed into another kind of landscape entirely. The city dropped away quickly. In its place came wind, sun, boulders, long views, and the particular silence of the desert — not empty, exactly, but spacious.

The trail was less a single path than a conversation with the land. We moved through washes and around rock formations, pausing often to look back at where we had come from and ahead toward the border country stretching out in every direction. Everything seemed exposed and hidden at once: the wide sky, the tucked-away campsites, the marks left by people who had passed through long before us.

One of the most memorable moments was coming across the petroglyph. It was a reminder that this place has never really been empty wilderness in the way people sometimes imagine the desert. It has been traveled, known, marked, and remembered. Standing there, dusty and sunburned and carrying too much backpacking food, I felt the strange compression of time that certain places can create.

The desert did what the desert often does: it made everything feel both stripped down and more vivid.

That evening, the three of us made camp and watched the light change across the rocks. The day’s heat softened, the shadows lengthened, and the sky became the main event. There is a particular pleasure in reaching camp with friends: the small rituals of unrolling sleeping pads, comparing snacks, making dinner, and pretending that the ground is more comfortable than it is.

Overnight, the desert felt larger. Sounds traveled differently. The stars felt closer. By morning, the trip had already taken on that slightly mythic quality that short adventures sometimes do — only one night, not far from home, but somehow expanded by the landscape, the company, and the feeling of having stepped briefly outside the usual rhythm of life.
